# Students as Sovereign Participants or Peasant Users?

## Whichever Vendor Controls the AI in Classrooms Controls the World

We stand at the crossroads where artificial intelligence meets the very purpose of education—and the path we've been walking is already a capitulation. This isn't a future crisis. It's a present failure we've been living inside so long we forgot to name it. The moment we handed children screens running proprietary operating systems, learning through proprietary software, generating data for proprietary models, we had already failed them. Before the first AI chatbot entered a classroom, the enclosure was complete. We taught an entire generation to be tenants in their own minds, grateful for access to tools they'll never own, trained to click "I agree" without reading, without understanding, without any expectation that understanding was even possible. That's not digital literacy. That's digital serfdom with a friendly interface.

Now the stakes compound. Artificial intelligence isn't just another tool in the classroom—it's a mirror and a mold for cognition itself. When a child learns to think _with_ an AI, the architecture of that AI shapes the architecture of that thinking. Who builds the model? Who owns the weights? Who decides what it reinforces, what it forgets, what it refuses to say? These aren't technical questions. They're political questions wearing technical clothes. And right now, the answers are: corporations build it, corporations own it, corporations decide. We're not augmenting intelligence. We're outsourcing it to entities whose fiduciary duty is to shareholders, not students. The call is coming from inside the capitalist capture of cognition itself.

But here's the thing about crossroads—they imply a choice. We can still take the other path. As algorithmic systems scale from capturing our attention to capturing our reasoning, as capital metabolizes every commons it touches into private extraction, education becomes the most important site of genuine _re-public_. Not republic as nostalgia for some imagined past, but re-public as active verb: to make public again what has been enclosed. To raise humans who know the difference between using a tool and being used by one. To build institutions that model sovereignty so that children grow up believing sovereignty is possible. The classroom is where we decide whether the next generation inherits a commons or a company store.

Sovereignty isn't nostalgia. It's survival. It's also, frankly, easier than the alternative—easier than endless vendor negotiations, easier than explaining data breaches, easier than teaching kids to navigate five different proprietary platforms that will all be deprecated by the time they graduate. Open source means we own it. Local AI means the conversation stays in the building. Public infrastructure means the public controls it. The bicycle for the mind was never supposed to come with a lock we don't hold the key to. It's time to cut the lock. It's time to teach children that the tools of thought belong to the people who think.
